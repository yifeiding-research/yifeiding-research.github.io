Political Influencers in Digital Democracy: A Comparative Analysis of Country Size, Language Structure, and Democratic Quality

This project pioneers a Multilevel Influence Framework to study how structural properties of political systems — national audience size, linguistic architecture, and democratic quality — shape political influencer behaviour and audience engagement. Using the 2024 European Parliament election cycle as a shared data window, I analyse over 1,800 political content items across YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, Facebook, and Twitter/X. A central methodological contribution is a cross-lingual calibration approach using mBERT and XLM-R to address measurement invariance across Luxembourgish, French, German, and Hungarian — including a low-resource language almost entirely absent from existing NLP benchmarks.

Structural Algorithmic Disadvantage: How Engagement-Optimising Systems Marginalise Minority-Language Political Actors

I identify and document a structural mechanism by which engagement-optimising recommendation systems systematically amplify content in large-audience languages over equivalent minority-language content — not through explicit bias, but through the structural properties of engagement-signal generation in small-audience linguistic communities. Luxembourgish-language political influencers serve as the primary empirical case.
